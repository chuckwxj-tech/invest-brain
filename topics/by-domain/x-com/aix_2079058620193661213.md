## 原文

The most important sentence in the largest WF6 六氟化钨 producer in China,CSSC PERIC 中船特气 H1 2026 report:

"On the international front, leveraging the supply chain substitution window brought about by the high demand for tungsten hexafluoride, we will further increase the supply to leading overseas customers and orderly implement multiple long-term supply agreements."

Supply chain substitution window. Those three words are the whole thesis, stated by the company itself. CSSC sees the Japan-to-China WF6 shift as a structural window, and they're moving to lock in overseas customers before it closes.

Picking golden nuggets across the 26H1 report:

>Revenue: 1.9 billion RMB, up 83% YoY;
>Net profit: 348M, up 96%
>Q2 alone did 1.2b RMB in revenue and 247M in net profit = 170% YoY growth, which accelerated massively from Q1. 
>WF6 revenue specifically was up nearly 3x. Officially the main revenue driver.

>2,000 t/yr WF6 capacity now 
>1,000 t/yr expansion ready in 2027, making it the largest WF6 producer globally
>Customers already include TSMC, Micron, Kioxia, Intel, SMIC, and YMTC = Almost all leading semiconductor fabs

My thesis of WF6 supply chain shifting is playing out exactly as mentioned. Prior to export control in January 2026, WF6 was a Japan-dominated supply chain. CSSC was a side player.

Post export control world, CSSC will become the main  WF6 player.

Reading between the lines on how Tungsten has performed (and affected) downstream:

> 32.72% gross margin in Q2, up from 30.2% in Q1.
Most would agree that's pretty hacking decent? 
Honestly, it should have been higher. 

The price for high-purity tungsten powder, the upstream feedstock for WF6, spiked in 1H26, and CSSC's margin got squeezed. Their operating cashflow flipped to -241M RMB (almost a 600M swing from last period). 

Let that sink in: a company growing revenue 83% YoY, with customers lining up, had negative operating cashflow.

All because of Tungsten price in China!

All tungsten investors know that China's Tungsten prices have been trading at a much lower level than ex-China.

Yet they had to pay high prices to lock in tungsten powder supply and squeezed their margin. The downstream demand is there.

While I can't say WF6 had a full bottleneck in Tungsten powder(possibly only in high grade W powder), I can confidently say Japanese WF6's profits & operating cashflow won't be as pretty.

Ex-China Tungsten prices are trading substantially higher, with no sight of it easing.

They've had to, and probably have to continue to pay top $ to secure supply, that's if they're available.

This was exactly the dynamic I've been flagging. The WF6 supply chain is slowly shifting from Japan to China. This is a direct response & feedback from fabs who require WF6 to produce wafers.

I'm talking about high-purity tungsten powder, the grade needed for semiconductor-grade WF6. China controls the tungsten upstream, but the refining capacity for the purity levels these fabs demand is still tight, and concentrated in China. 

CSSC is the largest WF6 producer in China, running 2,000 tons of capacity, serving every household name fabs, and they still had to burn cash to secure Tungsten supply. That tells you everything about where the real constraint sits.

The outlook is actually constructive. Tungsten powder prices have started easing into Q3. WF6 spot prices are accelerating, driven by the same supply-demand gap that makes CSSC's capacity so valuable.If China's tungsten price remains where it is, H2 numbers could be significantly better than H1. 

However, this case would never apply to anyone outside of China. All ex-China WF6 producers will have to eat up the high ex-China Tungsten price, either suffering in profit margin themselves, or pass it on to their customers.

In other words, the ex-China Tungsten industry won't have it as easy as China has it.

There's also no guarantee whether ex-China WF6 producers will be able to secure enough feed to purchase either - and the fabs have responded by talking directly with CSSC to secure WF6 supplies.

It's the clearest signal there is when the fabs are reacting  as if there's a likelihood of their WF6 supply being disrupted.
