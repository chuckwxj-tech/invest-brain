## 原文

AI COST CURVE AND BUBBLE FRAMEWORK

Chey estimates that token cost has declined to approximately 20 percent-10 percent of its level 3 years earlier. The direction is plausible, but the comparison lacks a normalized denominator. Token cost varies by model quality, parameter count, precision, context length, latency target, batch size, utilization, hardware generation, input-output mix, and energy price. A raw dollar-per-token comparison can overstate progress if model capability or service quality changes.

The cost curve is nevertheless central to the demand thesis. Hardware throughput, HBM bandwidth, lower precision, sparsity, mixture-of-experts models, distillation, batching, optimized kernels, scheduling, networking, and power efficiency can reduce token cost. SK hynix participates through bandwidth, capacity, power efficiency, memory hierarchy, and reductions in data movement.

The bubble discussion is appropriately separated into technology reality and financial-market pricing. AI technology can generate real productivity gains while securities, private valuations, and capital expenditures overshoot. Historical infrastructure cycles frequently combine genuine technological adoption with excess capital formation. The existence of a bubble in selected assets would not establish that AI is commercially invalid.

For SK hynix, the relevant bear case is not a collapse to 0 AI demand. A more realistic bear case is that accelerator and memory capacity grow faster than monetized AI workloads, while software efficiency reduces memory intensity and Samsung and Micron qualify competitive products. Under that scenario, HBM ASPs, standard-DRAM pricing, margins, and utilization could decline even as global AI token volume continues increasing.

The listing itself illustrates this distinction. More than 7x subscription demand and a 12.8 percent initial-day increase indicate intense investor appetite. They do not resolve whether the marginal AI data-center project earns an attractive return or whether SK hynix can sustain a 72 percent operating margin. Capital-market demand and end-market return on capital are different variables.

MANAGEMENT CREDIBILITY AND SIGNAL VALUE

Chey’s credibility is supported by the 2012 acquisition of Hynix, which was widely viewed as financially risky because of the target’s distressed history, capital intensity, and exposure to the memory cycle. The acquisition ultimately positioned SK Group at the center of the AI memory market and represents a highly successful long-duration strategic decision. (Reuters)

Past success improves confidence in strategic pattern recognition, willingness to invest countercyclically, and understanding of manufacturing complexity. It does not validate every current forecast. The capital requirements, geopolitical environment, customer concentration, infrastructure scale, and valuation starting point are now materially different from 2012.

The highest-confidence messages are that AI makes memory more strategically important, SK hynix has a genuine HBM advantage, power and talent are critical constraints, U.S. capital-market access is useful, and inference expands the importance of memory hierarchy. Medium-confidence messages include persistent supply tightness through 2027-2030, reduced cyclicality from long-term agreements, and expansion of memory value through CXL, NAND, software, and system design. Lower-confidence messages include 4x-6x customer capacity requirements, supply never catching demand until AGI, elimination of cyclicality, the precise 20GW data-center target, and a comparable 5x-10x decline in token cost.

Chey’s refusal to frame strategy as “catching Samsung” is consistent with an absolute stakeholder orientation rather than relative market-share targeting. That orientation can support customer relationships, employee retention, supplier trust, and long-term investment. It can also create minority-shareholder risk if capacity, employment, national-policy, or group-level objectives are prioritized over return on capital.

The commitment to “keep the stock price stable” should not be treated as operational guidance. Management controls strategy, disclosure, execution, capital allocation, and governance; it does not control market pricing. Repeated emphasis on share-price stability can also indicate sensitivity to the need for future issuance, employee-compensation value, or investor confidence.

INVESTMENT COMMITTEE SCENARIO FRAMEWORK

STRUCTURAL BULL CASE. HBM and broader AI memory remain structurally undersupplied beyond 2030. SK hynix sustains HBM share above 50 percent through HBM4, HBM4E, 16-high stacks, custom base dies, superior yields, and strong customer qualification. Binding long-term agreements provide volume floors, price protection, and prepayments. Inference and agentic workloads expand faster than memory-efficiency improvements. CXL, enterprise SSDs, Solidigm, and memory-system software increase addressable value. Operating margins normalize below 72 percent but remain substantially above historical memory-cycle averages. Incremental capacity earns returns materially above the cost of capital.

BASE CASE. Memory remains tight through 2027-2028, followed by progressive supply normalization. SK hynix remains the leading HBM supplier, but share declines as Micron and Samsung expand qualified capacity. HBM4 and custom products preserve premium economics, while conventional DRAM and NAND remain cyclical. Long-term agreements reduce volatility but do not eliminate renegotiation or pricing risk. Operating margins fall materially from Q1 2026, but free cash flow remains positive after elevated capital expenditures. The U.S. listing improves access and visibility without fully closing the valuation gap.

BEAR CASE. Hyperscaler capital expenditure slows as AI monetization lags infrastructure investment. Software efficiency, smaller models, grouped-query attention, cache compression, and tiered memory reduce HBM bytes required per token faster than workloads expand. Samsung and Micron qualify competitive HBM4 and HBM4E products, compressing SK hynix share and ASP. M15X, Yongin, competitor fabs, and packaging expansion arrive during 2028-2030, creating excess conventional-DRAM capacity. China restrictions, TSMC concentration, power constraints, and group-level capital commitments reduce returns. Peak earnings contract sharply, revealing the 5.5x forward P/E as a peak-denominator valuation rather than a durable discount.

KEY MONITORING VARIABLES

The most important operating variables are HBM4 and HBM4E customer qualifications, package yield, stack yield, power efficiency, bandwidth, production volume, and SK hynix’s share relative to the current approximate 58 percent level. Additional indicators include 12-high and 16-high mix, custom base-die economics, TSMC capacity, CoWoS availability, substrate constraints, and delivered HBM revenue per wafer.

Contract quality should be assessed through disclosed duration, minimum volume, take-or-pay clauses, prepayments, cancellation rights, price floors, indexation, technology transitions, and customer concentration. Reported demand should be separated from binding backlog, allocation requests, forecasts, and non-binding memoranda.

Financial monitoring should emphasize DRAM and NAND bit shipments, ASP, inventory, receivables, customer advances, capital expenditures, depreciation, free cash flow, net cash, shareholder returns, and future ADS issuance. The relationship between capital expenditure and revenue should be compared with the company’s mid-30 percent discipline framework.

Capacity milestones should include M15X ramp, Yongin construction, advanced packaging in Cheongju, Indiana construction and qualification, potential overseas wafer-fab decisions, EUV tool deliveries, and competitor additions in Korea, the U.S., Japan, Singapore, and Taiwan.

Group-level monitoring should focus on the ownership, partners, funding, preleases, power contracts, construction cost, and hurdle rates for the proposed SK Telecom data centers. Related-party transactions between SK hynix and other SK entities require particular scrutiny.

Capital-market monitoring should include the ADS-to-Korean-common parity after adjusting for the 10:1 ratio and foreign exchange, depositary fees, conversion mechanics, liquidity, options development, passive-index eligibility, stock-based compensation, lock-up expiration, and any indication of repeated primary issuance.

BOTTOM LINE

The Bloomberg interview presents a strategically coherent but aggressively framed thesis. AI is increasing the strategic importance of memory, SK hynix has a genuine HBM advantage, and inference can create substantial demand for capacity, bandwidth, persistence, and memory hierarchy. The U.S. listing broadens capital access, improves U.S. alignment, supports global talent acquisition, and raises a large amount of capital with approximately 2.45 percent dilution.

The transaction is not a substitute for capital discipline. Gross proceeds are exceptionally material relative to the standalone balance sheet, while current profitability reflects scarcity conditions that should not be extrapolated indefinitely. The 72 percent Q1 operating margin, 680 percent trailing share-price increase, and 5.5x forward P/E collectively describe an enterprise with extraordinary earnings momentum and substantial peak-cycle risk.

The durable investment thesis requires SK hynix to defend its HBM4 position as Samsung and Micron qualify competing products, convert customer demand into enforceable multi-year economics, expand capacity without creating future oversupply, and prevent group-level infrastructure ambitions from diluting semiconductor returns. The structural AI-memory thesis is strong. The assertion that memory cyclicality has ended is not sufficiently supported.

The decisive underwriting question is whether normalized post-capital-expenditure free cash flow remains structurally above historical levels after HBM share normalizes, competitor capacity expands, and current scarcity pricing fades. The most significant downside is not the disappearance of AI demand. It is the normalization of pricing, margins, and market share before elevated capital commitments earn the expected return.
