# TSMC $TSM raised everything today: full-year revenue growth guidance from 30%+ to slightly above 40%, capex from $52–56B to $60–64B, and its US commitment by another $100B. Five...

## 原文

TSMC $TSM raised everything today: full-year revenue growth guidance from 30%+ to slightly above 40%, capex from $52–56B to $60–64B, and its US commitment by another $100B. Five takeaways, then the full memo and complete Q&A.

1. Gross margin reached 67.7%, above the top end of guidance at 67.5%. Annualized ROE was 45.9%. Inventory days rose by 7 days to 87, primarily due to preparations for the N2 ramp.

2. 2026 capex was raised from $52–56B to $60–64B. Management attributed the increase to higher customer demand and equipment price inflation.

3. TSMC added another $100B to its Arizona commitment, bringing the total to $265B. The new investment will likely add about four front-end and back-end fabs, including advanced packaging. TSMC also plans to build 13 leading-edge and advanced packaging fabs in Taiwan over the next several years.

4. An analyst asked whether unconstrained N3 demand was 30–50% above supply. C.C. Wei declined to endorse the estimate and only said the gap was “very big.” At the technology symposium, TSMC had said N2 capacity would grow at roughly a 70% CAGR from 2026 to 2028, while N3/N5 capacity would grow at roughly 25%. This time, Wei’s answer was simply: “Now it’s bigger.”

5. The steep N2 ramp will dilute gross margin by 3–4 percentage points in 3Q26 and 2H26. TSMC still guided 3Q26 gross margin to 65–67%. Overseas fabs will dilute gross margin by 2–3 points in the early stages, widening to 3–4 points later.

Full memo:

1. 2Q26 financials: Revenue was $40.2B, +12.0% QoQ and +33.7% YoY, at the top end of guidance. Gross margin was 67.7% versus guidance of 65.5–67.5%. Operating margin was 60.3% versus guidance of 56.5–58.5%. Net income was NT$706.6B, +77.4% YoY. EPS was NT$27.25, net margin 55.6%, and annualized ROE 45.9%. Cash and marketable securities totaled NT$3.5T, or about $110B. Q2 capex was $15.7B, bringing 1H26 capex to about $26.8B. Inventory days increased by 7 days to 87, mainly due to the N2 ramp.

2. Revenue mix: N2 contributed 3% of wafer revenue for the first time, followed by N3 at 30%, N5 at 33%, and N7 at 11%. N7 and below accounted for 77% in total. Based on the revenue mix, N2 revenue is estimated to have exceeded $1B. By platform, HPC was 66% of revenue, +20% QoQ; smartphone 22%, -4%; IoT 5%, +4%; automotive 4%, +15%; and DCE 1%, +5%.

3. 3Q26 guidance: Revenue of $44.6–45.8B, +12% QoQ and +37% YoY at the midpoint. Gross margin of 65–67%, operating margin of 56–58%, based on an FX assumption of NT$32.0 per US dollar.

4. Full-year 2026: USD revenue growth was raised from more than 30% to slightly above 40%. Capex was raised from $52–56B to $60–64B. Of the total, 70–80% will go to advanced process technologies, about 10% to specialty technologies, and 10–20% to advanced packaging, testing, mask making, and other items.

5. Margin structure: The N2 ramp will dilute gross margin by 3–4 percentage points in 3Q26 and 2H26. Overseas fabs will dilute gross margin by 2–3 points in the early stages and 3–4 points later. Q2 gross margin exceeded guidance due to cost improvements and higher utilization, partly offset by overseas fab dilution.

6. Capacity expansion: The additional $100B brings total Arizona investment to $265B. TSMC expects to add about four front-end and back-end fabs for N2 and below and advanced packaging, with timing tied to customer demand. TSMC also plans 13 leading-edge and advanced packaging fabs in Taiwan. Three additional N3 fabs are planned across Taiwan, Arizona, and Japan, while N5 tools in Taiwan continue to be converted to support N3.

7. Demand verification: TSMC does not simply add up every customer forecast. C.C. Wei said, “I believe every customer tells me the truth. But all the truths together is not a truth.” The company uses both bottom-up and top-down checks and tracks AI data center locations, construction progress, demand, and power availability to make sure its chips do not end up in inventory. Wei expects strong demand through 2029–2030, while acknowledging there could be a dip along the way.

8. Technology roadmap: A14 delivers 10–15% more speed at the same power, or 25–30% lower power at the same speed, with close to 20% higher chip density versus N2. Risk production starts in 2027 and volume production in 2028. A13 uses a 97% optical shrink to save more than 6% die area. A12 brings Super Power Rail to the A14 platform. Both A13 and A12 are scheduled for volume production in 2029. High-NA EUV adoption still depends on cost, maturity, and die-stitching issues caused by the halved field size. No adoption timeline was given.

9. Advanced packaging: Capacity remains tight enough to limit some customer shipments. TSMC welcomes alternatives such as Intel’s EMIB-T because they allow more front-end wafers to be packaged and shipped. Its glass substrate/core pilot line is under construction and needs about another year to mature. COUPE is already in production and should become an important technology over the next few years. TSMC will not break out advanced packaging capex because bottlenecks can move between packaging, testing, and front-end equipment.

10. Mature nodes: Shortages are concentrated in AI-related power management ICs and sensors. Consumer-related mature-node demand remains soft. JASM Fab 1 in Japan is adding CMOS image sensor capacity, while ESMC in Germany is focused on automotive and industrial applications.

Q&A:

Q1: Can TSMC provide a three-year capex outlook after raising 2026 capex?

A: No specific number. Last quarter, management said capex over the next three years would be “significantly higher than the past three years.” This quarter, the wording changed to “even more significantly higher.”

Q2: How will the $265B Arizona investment be deployed?

A: TSMC expects to add about four fabs across front-end manufacturing and back-end packaging. The company has an internal plan, but timing will depend on market conditions and customer demand.

Q3: Samsung has memory profits and Intel has US policy support. How does TSMC respond to foundry competition?

A: Wei said he envies how much money the Korean competitor is making and acknowledged that government support and capital help. But foundry still comes down to technology, manufacturing, and customer trust. “Choosing a technology partner is not buying milk from 7-Eleven.” Qualification and ramp take years.

Q4: Is the five-year AI accelerator revenue CAGR now above the previous mid-to-high 50% range?

A: No updated number because demand continues to rise. Wei’s answer was: “Stronger and stronger and stronger.”

Q5: Does Intel EMIB-T threaten TSMC’s advanced packaging business?

A: Packaging capacity is tight enough to limit customer growth, so TSMC welcomes more options. “We hope they will be successful.” More available packaging also allows TSMC to ship more front-end wafers.

Q6: What if customers ask TSMC to support other packaging technologies?

A: Customer success comes first. TSMC will support whatever helps customers bring their products to market.

Q7: Does capacity planning consider competitors’ expansion, or only customer demand?

A: Competition is always considered. TSMC then uses customer and CSP data for bottom-up and top-down assessments. Every customer may provide an honest forecast, but adding all forecasts together does not represent actual market demand.

Q8: How are data center power constraints and construction delays included in capacity planning?

A: TSMC tracks data center locations, construction progress, demand, and power availability to make sure delivered chips do not sit in inventory.

Q9: How long will supply remain tight?

A: Wei expects strong demand through 2029–2030, though he is not certain there will be no short-term dip along the way.

Q10: Memory makers are earning higher margins. Should TSMC raise prices more aggressively during the shortage?

A: Higher margins are welcome, but TSMC will not suddenly raise prices by 4–5x and leave customers unable to compete. Pricing should reflect TSMC’s value while still allowing customers to succeed and TSMC to fund long-term expansion.

Q11: Is rising AI customer concentration a concern?

A: No. Large customers are growing, but new AI players are also entering the market.

Q12: Would TSMC invest in or finance customers and customers’ customers?

A: “So far, no.” The current customer model is working smoothly, and TSMC does not plan to use this type of financial arrangement for now.

Q13: Will the additional $100B in US investment be completed in three years or five?

A: TSMC has a plan but no fixed schedule. The pace will depend on customer demand, and the company will move as fast as possible. The same applies to new fabs in Taiwan and Japan.

Q14: When will COUPE make a meaningful revenue contribution?

A: Production has started and will ramp over time. AI data centers need lower power and higher bandwidth, so management expects COUPE to become an important technology over the next few years.

Q15: Which AI category is growing faster: CPU, GPU, or XPU?

A: No detailed split was provided. Most of these customers use TSMC and rely on leading-edge nodes. TSMC is working with customers to allocate wafers across CPU, GPU, and XPU demand.

Q16: What is the status of glass core, glass substrate, and glass carrier technologies?

A: TSMC is developing lower-cost alternatives with substrate suppliers. The pilot line is under construction and needs about another year to mature before customer production.

Q17: What will drive revenue and capex over the next several years?

A: Management did not quantify future revenue growth and only said the next few years should be “very good business.” Capex is being driven by cloud AI, edge AI, CPUs, and accelerators.

Q18: Could Intel and other back-end packaging competitors use packaging as an entry point into front-end wafers?

A: TSMC views front-end wafer manufacturing and back-end packaging as different capabilities. With packaging still undersupplied, more back-end options help TSMC ship front-end wafers.

Q19: Will High-NA EUV adoption be delayed by the halved field size and die stitching?

A: TSMC already includes the smaller field size in its manufacturing cost assessment. Adoption will depend on cost and technical maturity. The company is working with ASML but has not named an adoption node.

Q20: Is unconstrained N3 demand 30–50% above supply, or even higher?

A: “Those are your numbers.” TSMC did not provide an estimate and only said the gap is “very big.”

Q21: At the technology symposium, TSMC showed roughly 70% CAGR for N2 capacity and 25% for N3/N5. Are those assumptions still valid?

A: Wei’s answer was: “Now it’s bigger.”

Q22: How much of the 10–20% back-end capex bucket goes specifically to advanced packaging?

A: TSMC will not break it out. Bottlenecks can appear in packaging, testing, or front-end tools, so the company wants flexibility to direct capex wherever it is needed.

Q23: Capex has increased by almost $10B since the start of the year. What changed?

A: First, customers continued to raise demand and push TSMC to expand capacity. Second, equipment prices increased with inflation.

Q24: Are mature nodes also broadly undersupplied?

A: No. Shortages are concentrated in power management ICs and sensors used in AI infrastructure. Demand in other consumer-related segments remains soft.
