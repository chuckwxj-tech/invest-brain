## 原文

$PENG closed the books on its convertible notes Friday, and there's a specific reason this capital matters right now.

Final tally: $750M total, the full $100M upsize option exercised, 0.00% coupon, 50% conversion premium, notes due 2031. Oversubscribed enough that the banks used every bit of the greenshoe. 

CEO Kash Shaikh framed it as extending debt maturities, cutting cash interest expense, and funding the AI Factory Platform strategy, capital efficient, in his words, while demand for memory and AI infrastructure accelerates.

Here's the opportunity that capital is actually pointed at.

Reporting this week described a real dilemma at Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron. All three wanted to sell integrated CXL modules, proprietary controller and DRAM bundled as one high-priced product. 

Data-center customers wanted the opposite, a disaggregated setup with the CXL controller separate and cheap commodity DRAM slotting in behind it.

Problem is, pushing that version hard cannibalizes DIMMs, the big three's largest existing revenue source, so all three have quietly pulled back rather than compete with their own cash cow.

Penguin doesn't have that conflict. SMART Modular, its memory division, has been building exactly the disaggregated architecture the market wants for years, CXL Add-in Cards with a separate controller that accept standard DDR5 RDIMMs. No commodity DIMM business to protect means no internal tension slowing them down. 

While the big three run cannibalization math before every decision, Penguin can just build the thing customers are actually asking for.

Zero-coupon capital, extended runway, and a structural lane the largest memory makers are proving reluctant to chase themselves. That's the setup.
