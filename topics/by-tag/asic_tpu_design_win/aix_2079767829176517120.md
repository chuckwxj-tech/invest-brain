$INTC Intel pre-Q2 earnings notes

Sat on my notes for a few days to see if there’ll be any new news ahead of Q2 earnings. Saw Intel secured a design win on Intel 4 (a mature node) with Fortinet (for SP6), and some rumors about Hynix looking to acquire Intel’s Ohio fabs (which has been debunked, more on this below)

• 18A node is ramping up nicely alongside yield improvements

18A Q2 yield is reportedly ~85% (up from 65% in Q1), right behind TSMC N2 (90%) and far ahead of Samsung SF2 (50-60%)

Panther Lake (Core Ultra Series 3) is currently the main driver for this ramp. Q2 product shipment volume is expected to be 6 - 7 times that of Q1. Huge ramp. That said, Panther Lake gross margin is still below group average gross margin but Q2 group gross margin is likely to be in the ballpark of Q1. Will take a few quarters before product gross margin catches up

Other products that’ll also contribute to the 18A ramp, more evident starting 2H 26:

+ Nova Lake (Core Ultra Series 4): 28-core DS (dual-compute tiles) to debut in Q1 27 (Jan-Mar), followed by the 28-core K-series (Mar-Apr ’27), 16-core and 8-core variants (late-Mar to May ’27). Flagship 52-core DS model to debut in late Q2-Q3 27 (late May-Sep)

+ Wildcat Lake (Core Series 3): launched on Apr 16, 2026

+ Clearwater Forest (Xeon 6+, E-cores): announced and commercial rollout on June 1, 2026

+ Microsoft Maia 2 (amongst others, likely)

Intel Fab 52 at Chandler, AZ is the core fab for 18A, capable of >10,000 18A wafer starts per week (~40-50K WSPM). This is combined with some capacity at D1X at Hillsboro, OR.

D1X is Intel’s flagship R&D fab so some early production on 18A was done there but at low volumes. It also houses ASML’s High-NA EUV machines on which a subset of Panther Lake chips is being produced. 14A node will also be developed at D1X with risk production targeted in 2028 before transitioning into high-volume manufacturing in 2029 (in Mod 1 and Mod 2 in New Albany, Ohio) – assuming if Intel has secured enough volume commitments, or Fab 62 (in Chandler, AZ) as stopgap

More on the fabs shortly

• 18A-P currently in risk production at D1X

18A-P is a performance-enhanced variant of, and is design rule-compatible with 18A

This means capital investments made in 18A can be extended to 18A-P – i.e. any additional capex for 18A-P is incremental

Internal products slated on 18A-P:

+ Diamond Rapids (Xeon 7, P-cores): launching in 2027. More specs to be disclosed at Hot Chips (Aug 23 – 25)

+ Crescent Island: Intel’s data center GPU. The product was first announced on Oct 14, 2025. It’s built on Intel’s Xe3P GPU architecture, uses LPDDR5X (vs GDDR/HBM) and a wide-and-slow approach (using 640-bit bus x 20) so likely to target for on-prem inference deployment. Early customer testing and sampling is scheduled in H2 2026 with full rollout in 2027

+ External customers: Apple M-series chips, Microsoft Maia 3 and potentially others. I noted some brokers have indicated secured design wins from AMD, Nvidia, Marvell, Microsoft, Micron, OpenAI, but none of these companies have publicly confirmed specific foundry contract with Intel as well as nature of engagement (production orders, design-in evaluations, wafer runs, early-stage collaboration, etc)

• Intel 4/Intel 3 as mature nodes continue to ship products

For those new to Intel’s foundry process, which is bifurcated:

1) RibbonFET (GAA) + backside power delivery (BSPD): 18A, 18A-P, 14A, 14A-E, etc. Gen1 PowerVia and Gen2 PowerDirect are two sequential generations of Intel’s BSPD. 18A, 18A-P use PowerVia whereas 14A, 14A-E will deploy PowerDirect delivery architecture

2) FinFET: Intel 4 / Intel 3 (3-nm class), as well as mature nodes (Intel 7/16/16-E, and UMC-Intel 12 which is 12nm-class). Intel 3 plays a crucial role in bridging FinFET and RibbonFET

Intel primarily uses Intel 3 for its internal products – especially Granite Rapids (Xeon 6). Granite Rapids had been batch-launched since Sep 2024, and the latest variant Granite Rapids-WS (Xeon 600) was launched on Feb 2, 2026

Intel recently announced (on July 13) that they began a €5b capital investment to upgrade Fab 34 (in Leixlip, Ireland) which they acquired the 49% equity stake from Apollo in April. The investment shall maximize capacity for Intel 3 that will deliver Xeon 6 and next-gen Xeon processors

The partnership with Fortinet will also see the latter’s next-gen chips (SP6) to be manufactured on Intel 4

• 14A – a key process node to track

Intel claimed that 14A maturity (yield and performance) has outpaced 18A at a comparable stage in terms of development timeline. They had secured TeraFab as their anchor customer and are in engagements with customers on PDK/trial runs. Key milestone is 14A v0.9 PDK expected around October 2026

That said, test production with TeraFab is a few years out (risk production earliest in 2028). Internal product evaluations may be earlier, but Intel needs more secured design wins for 14A to be viable

This brings me to the next section – Fabs. Shall focus on a few key ones

As mentioned, Fab 52 (Arizona) has been ramping (since Oct 2025) with ~40-50K WSPM. This fab has mostly been assigned to 18A/18A-P

Fab 62 (Arizona) is 18A/18A-P capable but assignment is TBC. Construction is underway and expected to be completed around 2028. This fab may be a potential stopgap for 14A if the Ohio fabs aren’t ready, or as additional 18A/18A-P capacity if external demand ramps

The Hillsboro, Oregon fabs have already been discussed (D1X being their flagship development fab, amongst others)

Now, on the fabs in New Albany, Ohio

Mod 1 and Mod 2 are intended to be assigned to 14A and future nodes. Both are currently under construction, with Mod 1 scheduled for operations in 2030-2031, and Mod 2 in 2032

The Ohio fab projects are the most problematic (on paper at least). They broke ground in 2022 on $28b Phase I originally targeting 2025 production but the schedule was reset in Feb last year

It’s unclear how much of cleanroom space would be assigned for TeraFab production but given the scale of the Ohio site (with capacity for up to 8 fabs), Intel needs volume commitment for 14A to be viable – a lot more than TeraFab’s design win

This is where a potential JV with SK Hynix may be interesting

Assuming if Intel enters into a partnership with SK Hynix similar to those with Apollo and Brookfield IP (the latter owns 49% of Fab 52 and 62), then the Ohio fab projects from Intel’s POV will be significantly de-risked

In return, SK Hynix gets 14A capacity allocation and cleanroom for the manufacturing of their next-gen HBM logic base dies (which also means some volumes re-assigned from TSMC)

On packaging – EMIB-T has reportedly reached 98% yield, matching TSMC’s CoWoS-L at 5.5x reticle size. There’s chatter that Rubin Ultra is encountering implementation issues on TSMC’s platform – if so, this may benefit EMIB-T, especially for next-gen products, amongst which reportedly include Nvidia’s Feynman GPU, Google’s TPU HumuFish and AWS’ Trainium3

Separately on Foveros 3D, Fab 11X (with Fab 9 adjacent) in Rio Rancho, New Mexico is going to be the main high-volume advanced packaging site in the US

Assembly and testing ramps are going to be evident in Malaysia. Intel’s site at Penang was originally placed on hold in early 2025 but has been revived, with operations set to commence in late 2026 with multi-year expansion plans. They are consolidating their Costa Rican A&T ops into Malaysia and Vietnam, with decommissioning to be completed by end 2026

Lastly, on financials/capital plans

Intel had guided Q2 non-GAAP group gross margin at 39% (below Q1 of 41% which already was 650bps ahead of guidance). IFS operating loss is expected to improve through the year as 18A volume ramps and yields improve across 18A and Intel 3/4, partly offset by higher opex associated with step-ups in 14A investments

ASPs are likely to be higher q-o-q. I don’t have solid numbers but would imagine ASP increases somewhere in the mid-teens across both server CPUs/mobile SoCs

Total planned gross capex for 2026 is expected to be flat y-o-y at around $17-18b, roughly equal across the year, and heavily weighted towards SPE (equipment)

On capital plans, Intel is committed to retiring all $2.5b in maturities coming due this year and $3.8b in 2027 (for a total of $6.3b)

Whether or not they need capital raise for a debt refi remains to be seen. If a JV with SK Hynix comes through, with partner capital contribution, then a capital raise is unlikely required. Otherwise, a cap raise in the form of debt could be possible. I believe investor appetite for Intel’s credit is robust – evident in the 7.7x book cover for its recent multi-tranche $6.5b debt raised in April
