## 原文

1/ The news, in one line. SK Hynix just ran the largest US listing ever by a foreign company. It listed ADRs on Nasdaq July 10 under SKHY, raised $26.5B, oversubscribed about 7x. In the interview chairman Chey Tae-won floated a phrase, memory as a service (MaaS): rent memory by capacity or bandwidth instead of buying chips outright. Then he added four words most people skipped. An idea at this point.

2/ The real story isn't renting memory. It's memory trying to escape its own curse. DRAM and HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) are the most brutal boom-bust in tech. Prices rip in an upcycle, then capacity floods in and margins fall off a cliff. MaaS wants to swap one-time chip sales for long-term contracts and recurring revenue. Push revenue visibility up and the multiple can drift from "cyclical" toward "subscription business." That re-rating is the actual prize.

3/ The market is taking a mental shortcut. It assumes memory subscriptions will work because storage subscriptions already do. Storage as a service (STaaS) is proven: Everpure (formerly Pure Storage) shipped Evergreen//One in 2018, and NetApp's Keystone grew subscription revenue about 65% in FY2026. But STaaS sells a whole system the customer already buys as a box. Memory is a part welded next to the GPU. HBM needs a thousand short wires pressed against the compute to hit its bandwidth, and CXL (Compute Express Link) pooling still runs 200 to 500 nanoseconds. Mapping a system-level win onto a component is representativeness bias.

4/ There are two roads to MaaS, and they are not equally hard. The financial road keeps the chips in place and changes only the contract. You rent by the TB per month, the maker keeps ownership, and it could be signed almost tomorrow with zero new hardware. The pooling road is a real CXL memory pool with capacity handed out by the GB-hour. That one is science fiction for now and needs the silicon and the ecosystem to grow up together. Chey himself says the model isn't defined, which tells you version one takes the easy road.

5/ The opportunity splits into two layers, on two different clocks. The first layer pays off soon: memory makers re-rated on long-term supply deals and this service narrative. The June multi-year pact with Nvidia is already the tell, and that thesis holds even if MaaS never ships. The second layer is slower: the real pooling buildout, when CXL controllers, switches and optical interconnect (the pick-and-shovel names) finally get their turn. But that revenue mostly lands in 2027 and beyond. Don't pay today for a 2028 story.
