## 原文

Someone Claimed They Diversified Away From China's Scandium Supply Chain.

Me being the guy who studies how Beijing is creating policies to mining landscape in China is of course piqued by the claim.

Let's look at some data - screenshots as the sources.

Someone claims they've fully diversified away from China's scandium dominance. On paper, it looks convincing. The United States is 100% import-reliant on scandium (USGS), of which 89% of those imports come from Japan. The remainder comes from China.

Clean supply chain and definitely doable, right? Japan delivers, problem solved.

Here's what the story says if you look deeper into the supply chain.

Japan's Feedstock Reality

China was Japan's largest supplier of mixed rare earth compounds in 2025. Not by a small margin. China is the dominant supplier. Japan's high-purity scandium oxide, which is typically separated from refining the imported MREC from China. You can also debate that Scandium comes as a by-product as nickel laterite. Indonesia is the largest nickel laterite producer but USGS isn't mentioning it, so I won't either.

The processing and purification happens in Japan. The value-add happens in Japan. All these are done before it goes off to downstream market like aerospace, defense and semiconductor.

But the origin does not change. The label on the box says Japan. What is inside the box is still Chinese.

The Three-Step Chain

This is the pattern across every critical mineral that matters. Raw material enters China. It gets processed, sometimes to intermediate compounds, sometimes to metal, sometimes to high-purity oxide. Then it ships to Japan for further purification. Then it reaches the West.

Three steps. China controls step one(mining & processing). Without step one, steps two and three do not exist.

If it is a critical mineral, it usually goes through China for processing before it goes anywhere else for purification. Japan is typically the second stop, but never the first.

Tungsten is a proven example

Tungsten is the working proof of concept. China's export controls on tungsten intermediates hit Japanese carbide tool manufacturers within months. Mitsubishi and Sumitomo issued price hike notices. Inventory buffers evaporated faster than anyone in Tokyo expected. WF6 supply chain was quick to put in a state of panic - causing semiconductor companies to reevaluate its supply chain in attempt to diversify it.

The same exercise is running across all critical minerals and rare earths right now. This applies to all minerals where China controls the front end of the processing chain.

The Bottom Line

You cannot diversify away from a supplier who controls the entire midstream. The scandium supply chain was never truly diversified. It was just one degree of separation away from the same choke point, wearing a Japanese label.

The January 6 China's Dual-use Items Export Control (Very important for everyone to understand)

You may ignore the previous facts. But if there's 1 thing to take away from this, this would be the section you want to read, and possibly re-read again.

On 6 January 2026, China implemented dual-use item export controls that effectively halted rare earth and critical mineral exports to Japan on announcement. This was not a tariff adjustment. This was not a licensing slowdown. This was a hard stop.

Why Japan specifically? Because Beijing understands something most Western supply chain maps miss completely: Japan is the purification chokepoint for critical minerals flowing to the West. If you control what enters Japan, you control what exits to the United States and Europe.

The January 6 controls are still in place. Everything suggests they stay in place. Since then, multiple policies and regulations have been revised, making it even stricter & harder to export.

It's obvious China will not be easing the grip, but to toughen the rules around critical minerals, and export controls that are "accordingly to laws & regulations" will be the buzz-sentence you'll be hearing from Beijing.
