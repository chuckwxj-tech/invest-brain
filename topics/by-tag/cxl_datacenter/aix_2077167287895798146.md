The original volume and page references are retained wherever legible. Several entries in the printed index combine many separate subjects beneath one heading, so I have divided them into individual knowledge-units. Spiritual, supernatural, and alchemical correspondences are presented as interpretive expansions when they are not explicit claims of Holland’s text.

C
94/XCIV. Julius Caesar on Early British Iron - Vol. I, p. 15
Caesar’s testimony concerning iron in Britain supplies one of Holland’s earliest literary witnesses to native metallurgy. Classical reporting, archaeological remains, regional ore, weapons, tools, and coinage are placed in dialogue. Hermeneutically, Caesar becomes an external reader of Britain’s material culture, interpreting a people partly through the metals they possessed, valued, traded, and transformed.
95/XCV. William Camden and British Metallic Antiquities - Vol. I, p. 16
William Camden’s antiquarian writings preserved notices of ancient Britain, Roman remains, regional industries, inscriptions, coins, and local traditions. His importance lies in joining text, landscape, artifact, and memory. Camden demonstrates an early archaeological hermeneutic in which buried metal, ruined works, place names, and chronicles mutually illuminate one another.
96/XCVI. Candlesticks - Vol. III, p. 367
Metal candlesticks unite illumination, domestic design, ecclesiastical symbolism, casting, spinning, swaging, soldering, chasing, and silver plating. Their structure must securely hold the candle while collecting wax and resisting heat. Spiritually, the candlestick becomes a bearer of ordered light, recalling temple lamps, sanctuary vessels, and the biblical image of illumination established upon a fashioned stand.
97/XCVII. Casting and Boring Cannon - Vol. I, p. 56
Cannon founding required enormous furnace heat, carefully prepared moulds, controlled metal composition, and accurate boring. A hidden flaw could become catastrophic under explosive pressure. The cannon represents metallurgy intensified into concentrated force, where casting, geometry, combustion, ballistics, and political power converge.
98/XCVIII. Earliest Unquestionable Use of Cannon - Vol. II, p. 86
The treatise associates the certain European use of cannon with the fourteenth century and mentions reports of artillery at Crécy in 1346. Early guns may have been cast brass tubes or cylinders constructed from longitudinal iron bars strengthened by hoops. The history shows weapon technology emerging gradually from older foundry, barrel-making, and pyrotechnical arts.
99/XCIX. Brass Cannon - Vol. III, p. 202
Brass or bronze cannon depended upon copper-tin alloys selected for castability, strength, and resistance to corrosion. Their manufacture required immense quantities of molten metal, large moulds, secure furnace operations, and later boring. Alchemically, the cannon is a paradoxical union of Venusian copper and Jovial tin transformed into an instrument of thunder.
100/C. Cannon as Artificial Thunder
The cannon imitates natural thunder through contained fire, expanding gas, metallic confinement, and sudden release. Older writers often interpreted artillery through elemental language. Earth supplied the metal, fire awakened the powder, air carried the report, and the projectile crossed terrestrial space. It became a manufactured storm under human command.
101/CI. Caratacus and the Metallic World of Ancient Britain - Vol. I, p. 16
Caratacus belongs to the historical setting of British resistance to Rome. His presence evokes native weapons, armour, chariots, iron tools, and regional smithcraft. In the index he functions as a historical marker connecting metallurgy with sovereignty, warfare, identity, and resistance to imperial incorporation.

102/CII. William Caslon and English Type Founding - Vol. III, p. 117
William Caslon refined English type founding and created letterforms admired for clarity, balance, and durability. His achievement required design, punch cutting, matrix making, alloy control, casting, dressing, and printing. Caslon’s work reveals a profound correspondence: written language depends upon microscopic architecture impressed into metal before it can be multiplied upon paper.
103/CIII. The Cassiterides or Tin Islands - Vol. III, pp. 2-3
The Cassiterides belong to the ancient geography of tin commerce and were associated with Britain or Atlantic islands supplying Mediterranean civilizations. They form a knowledge-lagoon joining Phoenician navigation, classical geography, Cornwall, bronze manufacture, secrecy of trade routes, and the movement of mineral wealth across seas.
104/CIV. Tin as an Agent of Bronze Civilization
The importance of the Cassiterides arises from tin’s ability to transform copper into bronze. A relatively small addition alters hardness, casting behaviour, colour, resonance, and utility. Tin therefore acts as a subtle modifier whose hidden power exceeds its quantity, an early example of how minute proportions govern the character of an alloy.
105/CV. Establishment of the Cast-Iron Foundry - Vol. I, p. 55
The cast-iron foundry arose from the ability to melt iron sufficiently for pouring into moulds. This required the blast furnace, powerful air supply, suitable fuel, fluxes, and moulding knowledge. Casting separated final form from direct hammering, allowing identical or complex shapes to emerge from prepared cavities.
106/CVI. Foundry as a Chamber of Negative Form
The mould contains an absence shaped like the future object. Molten metal enters this hollow and gives bodily existence to an invisible design. Hermeneutically, casting is an art of reading negative space. The completed object was first present as emptiness, proportion, boundary, and intended form.

107/CVII. Cast-Iron Press for Plate Printing - Vol. II, p. 236
The cast-iron roller press supplied rigidity and uniform pressure for copperplate or other forms of printing. Iron frames resisted distortion more effectively than wooden structures. The press united mechanical compression with visual transmission, making metal both the machine of reproduction and, through engraved plates, the carrier of the image.
108/CVIII. Casting Metal Pipes - Vol. I, p. 59
Metal pipes required long internal cores, accurately arranged moulds, proper wall thickness, and controlled pouring. Pipes created artificial subterranean or architectural veins for water, gas, steam, and other fluids. They imitate natural channels while disciplining flow according to human design.
109/CIX. William Caxton and Printing in England - Vol. III, p. 116
William Caxton is commonly credited with establishing printing with movable type in England. His inclusion demonstrates that literary history rests upon metallurgy. Type metal, presses, screws, ink balls, matrices, and tools enabled speech to survive distance and time. The printer became a mechanical multiplier of memory.
110/CX. Count de Caylus on Ancient Copper Hardening - Vol. III, p. 174
Anne Claude de Caylus investigated ancient artifacts and methods, including traditions concerning the hardening of copper. Before modern alloy analysis, exceptionally hard bronze implements were sometimes imagined to contain a lost tempering secret. The inquiry belongs to archaeometallurgy and the persistent lore of ancient technical superiority.
111/CXI. The Lost Art of Hardening Copper
Pure copper cannot be hardened like carbon steel, yet it becomes harder through cold working and far harder when alloyed with tin, arsenic, or other substances. Ancient “hardened copper” therefore opens questions concerning work hardening, alloying, impurities, quenching traditions, and misunderstood archaeological terminology.
112/CXII. Celtiberian Steel Preparation - Vol. I, p. 13
The Celtiberians were celebrated for sword blades made by carefully preparing iron and steel. Ancient reports describe prolonged treatment, selective corrosion, forging, or burial practices intended to remove weaker portions. Whether every story is literal or embellished, the tradition preserves a reputation for severe material selection and disciplined blade craft.
113/CXIII. Atavistic Blade Memory
Celtiberian steel belongs to an ancestral memory in which the sword was more than a tool. Its metal embodied lineage, regional identity, military honour, and the smith’s guarded knowledge. The blade became a material genealogy, transmitting technique across generations without necessarily preserving a written formula.

114/CXIV. Don Miguel Rufín de Celis and Meteoric Iron - Vol. I, p. 7
Don Miguel Rubín de Celis investigated the great iron mass of Campo del Cielo in South America. His observations helped establish that enormous metallic bodies could occur naturally and later be interpreted as meteorites. This entry joins travel, mineralogy, astronomy, colonial science, and celestial metallurgy.
115/CXV. Celestial Metal Hermeneutics
Meteoric iron demanded interpretation because it blurred distinctions between heaven and earth. Was it generated by lightning, volcanic force, subterranean chemistry, or celestial descent? Such masses became physical texts through which natural philosophers attempted to read the architecture of the cosmos.
116/CXVI. Chaff Cutters - Vol. II, p. 259
Chaff cutters used rotating or reciprocating blades to reduce straw and fodder into smaller pieces. Their construction required cutting geometry, feed control, guards, frames, cranks, and durable steel edges. They demonstrate the migration of blade technology from handheld tools into agricultural machinery.
117/CXVII. Chains, Various Descriptions - Vol. I, p. 179
Chains include trace chains, pit chains, cable chains, suspension chains, ornamental chains, and numerous specialized forms. Each converts discrete links into collective strength. No single link spans the required distance, but repeated interconnection creates flexibility, continuity, and tensile capacity.

118/CXVIII. Antiquity of Chains - Vol. I, p. 180
Chains appear in ancient warfare, imprisonment, marine practice, wells, gates, ceremonial objects, and sacred imagery. Their symbolic meanings include bondage, authority, continuity, covenant, and restraint. The same geometry may secure a ship, suspend a bridge, confine a prisoner, or carry ornament.
119/CXIX. Fabrication of Chains - Vol. I, p. 182
Chain making required cutting iron rods, bending them into links, heating their ends, welding them closed, and testing the finished sequence. A defective weld could compromise the entire chain. The craft therefore embodies collective integrity, where local soundness determines the safety of the whole.
120/CXX. Knotted Trace Chains - Vol. I, p. 183
Knotted trace chains were formed through particular link or joining arrangements adapted to harness and traction. Their purpose was to transmit animal force into carts, ploughs, or machinery. The chain mediates between living muscular energy and inert mechanical load.
121/CXXI. Cable Chains - Vol. I, p. 185
Iron chain cables replaced or supplemented hemp ropes aboard ships. They resisted rot, offered immense strength, and could be formed in standardized links. Their weight, corrosion, shock response, and reliability required careful calculation. Chain cable manufacture transformed naval safety through ferrous tenacity.
122/CXXII. Comparative Strength of Chains and Ropes - Vol. I, p. 191
The treatise compares chain and rope through weight, tensile capacity, elasticity, durability, cost, handling, and response to moisture. Rope absorbs shock differently, while chain offers resistance to decay and fire. This is an early comparative materials study, selecting substances according to operating conditions rather than prestige.
123/CXXIII. Chain as Hermeneutic Figure
The chain offers a useful model for reading the three volumes themselves. Ore, furnace, bar, link, machine, trade, and civilization form successive connections. Knowledge is not isolated in individual facts; it gains strength when historical, chemical, mechanical, and spiritual meanings are joined without breaking their distinctions.
124/CXXIV. Origin of Chimneys - Vol. II, p. 160
Chimneys reorganized domestic fire by separating smoke from inhabited space and establishing controlled draught. Their development involved architecture, combustion, pressure differences, masonry, weather, and household custom. The chimney is a vertical conduit joining subterranean fuel, terrestrial habitation, and the atmosphere above.
A
125/CXXV. Chimney as Artificial Axis
Symbolically, the chimney resembles an axis through which the products of earthly fire ascend. In practical terms, heated gases rise because of density differences. In hermeneutic terms, the structure mediates below and above, hearth and sky, hidden combustion and visible plume.
126/CXXVI. Chinese Method of Casting Sheet Lead - Vol. III, p. 66
The Chinese method of producing sheet lead differed from European rolling practices and may have used casting upon prepared surfaces or between layers. The entry preserves cross-cultural metallurgy and demonstrates that thin metallic sheets could be achieved through several technological pathways.
127/CXXVII. Chinese Gong - Vol. III, p. 192
The Chinese gong is a shaped bronze instrument whose complex sound depends upon alloy, curvature, thickness, hammering, and heat treatment. Unlike an ordinary bell, it radiates sound from a broad metallic disc. Its voice demonstrates the conversion of struck matter into expanding atmospheric waves.
128/CXXVIII. Gong as Sonic Cosmology
The gong’s sound seems to emerge from an entire surface rather than a single point. Its expanding resonance offers a material analogy for ordered vibration moving through space. Ancient ritual use adds a spiritual dimension, where sound marks transitions, summons attention, and establishes ceremonial atmosphere.
129/CXXIX. Christie’s Mangle - Vol. II, p. 258
Christie’s mangle improved the mechanical smoothing of cloth through rollers, pressure, gearing, or compact arrangement. It belongs to domestic mechanization and textile finishing. The device translates repetitive muscular labour into controlled compression.
130/CXXX. Christopher Schultz and Engine-Drawn Iron Wire - Vol. II, p. 329
Christopher Schultz is credited with introducing engine-powered iron wire drawing into England in 1565. The innovation replaced or augmented manual pulling with mechanical force. Wire drawing transforms a short, thick bar into a long, slender filament through repeated passage across diminishing apertures.
131/CXXXI. Wire Drawing as Metallic Attenuation
Wire drawing reveals a remarkable material mystery: metal can be extended enormously without ceasing to be continuous. The operation requires ductility, annealing, lubrication, hard drawplates, and measured reduction. It is an art of making strength inhabit slenderness.
132/CXXXII. Chronometers - Vol. III, p. 285
Chronometers were precision timekeepers essential to the determination of longitude at sea. Their springs, balances, escapements, pivots, jewels, temperature compensation, and finely finished metals had to resist motion, climate, and time itself. The chronometer is a portable mechanical cosmos governed by regulated recurrence.
133/CXXXIII. Chronometric Hermeneutics
A chronometer does not create time, but interprets it through periodic motion. Its reading depends upon the reliability of its internal correspondences. Wheel, spring, escapement, and balance translate invisible duration into visible indication, making time mechanically legible.
134/CXXXIV. Chubb’s Patent Lock - Vol. II, p. 272
Chubb’s lock employed levers and a detector mechanism intended to reveal or prevent unauthorized manipulation. Its security arose from hidden internal order and precision. The lock could in a limited sense “remember” interference by entering a condition that required the proper key or resetting procedure.
135/CXXXV. Durability and Security of Chubb’s Lock - Vol. II, p. 274
The lock was publicly tested by skilled mechanics and locksmiths. Its reputation depended upon resistance to picking, forcing, wear, and deceptive tools. Such trials resemble experimental philosophy, since a mechanical claim was subjected to adversarial demonstration.
136/CXXXVI. Combination Door Latch - Vol. II, p. 284
The combination latch integrated several motions or security functions into one compact arrangement. It demonstrates mechanical compression, where multiple operations are placed within a small housing. The latch becomes a threshold technology controlling passage between public and private space.
137/CXXXVII. Invention of Clocks - Vol. III, p. 283
The origin of mechanical clocks emerged from older sundials, water clocks, clepsydrae, candles, bells, and astronomical instruments. The mechanical clock transformed continuous natural processes into an enclosed sequence of weights, gears, escapement, and periodic release.
138/CXXXVIII. Changes in the Fashion of House Clocks - Vol. III, p. 284
House clocks changed from monumental and architectural forms to long-case, bracket, mantel, and compact designs. Fashion altered cases, dials, ornament, and placement, while mechanical principles continued beneath the changing exterior. The entry distinguishes technological essence from cultural clothing.
139/CXXXIX. Clock Manufactories - Vol. III, p. 285
Clock manufacture required the cooperation of wheel cutters, spring makers, dial painters, brass founders, case makers, engravers, finishers, and assemblers. A clock is therefore not the work of one craft but a coordinated republic of specialized intelligences.
140/CXL. Eight-Day Clock - Vol. III, p. 289
An eight-day clock stored sufficient energy in weights or springs to operate for approximately one week between windings. Its duration depended upon efficient gearing, regulated release, low friction, and adequate power reserve. It became a domestic governor of hours, labour, worship, meals, and sleep.
141/CXLI. Mechanism of Clocks - Vol. III, p. 291
Clock mechanism consists of a power source, wheel train, escapement, regulator, dial work, and often striking apparatus. Each part constrains the next. The clock is an image of ordered causality, in which freedom of motion is rendered useful through measured interruption.
142/CXLII. Clothes-Smoothing Irons - Vol. II, p. 253
Smoothing irons applied heated metal and pressure to cloth. Their design involved thermal mass, handles, surface finish, fuel, and protection from scorching. They embody a domestic alliance of heat, weight, and movement, making disorderly fibres appear smooth and socially presentable.
143/CXLIII. Coal, Its Abundance and Value - Vol. I, p. 42
Coal liberated iron manufacture from exclusive dependence upon charcoal and forests. Its transformation into coke enabled larger furnaces and more sustained heat. Yet coal also altered landscapes, labour, air quality, and urban growth. It is both concentrated subterranean energy and a stern reminder that industrial gifts carry environmental consequences.
144/CXLIV. Coke Burning - Vol. I, p. 41
Coking drives volatile matter from coal, leaving a carbon-rich fuel suited to blast furnaces. The operation resembles calcination: raw matter is purified by controlled fire before entering a greater transformation. Coke became the dark intermediary through which ore could be reduced on an unprecedented scale.
