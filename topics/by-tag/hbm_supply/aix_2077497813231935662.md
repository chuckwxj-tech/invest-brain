NVIDIA’s Next AI Opportunity: Lowering the Cost of Inference

An educational perspective on how AI infrastructure is shifting from maximizing compute to maximizing economic output.

The first phase of the generative AI boom was largely about building capacity. Hyperscalers and AI companies raced to acquire GPUs, construct data centers, and secure power. NVIDIA was the primary beneficiary because training increasingly capable AI models required enormous amounts of accelerated computing.

The next phase may be economically different.

As AI moves from model development toward large-scale deployment, the business question is shifting from:

How much AI compute can we build?

to:

How much useful AI inference can we generate from the infrastructure and power we already have?

This shift creates a new opportunity for NVIDIA—and potentially for companies such as Marvell that provide the connectivity and memory infrastructure required to make expensive AI compute more productive.

The Economics of Inference

Every interaction with an AI model consumes computing resources. As AI expands into reasoning models, coding assistants, autonomous agents, and enterprise applications, inference demand could grow dramatically.

The challenge is that two critical resources are increasingly constrained: power and memory.

For hyperscalers, the economic objective is becoming straightforward:

Generate more AI output from every dollar of infrastructure and every megawatt of available power.

One useful measure is tokens per watt—how much AI output can be generated within a fixed power envelope.

The higher the utilization of an expensive AI accelerator, the more potential revenue it can generate. Conversely, a GPU waiting for data or memory represents expensive capital that is not being fully monetized.

The Memory Wall

AI accelerator performance has been improving faster than memory capacity and bandwidth.

This matters because advanced inference workloads—particularly long-context reasoning and AI agents—create increasingly large KV caches, which store information the model has already processed.

The fastest place to keep this information is the GPU's HBM, but HBM is expensive and limited. When the required context exceeds available HBM, the system may need to move information to slower storage or recompute it. Both can reduce GPU utilization.

From a business perspective, this creates an unattractive situation:

Companies are buying increasingly powerful and expensive GPUs, but memory constraints can prevent those GPUs from operating at their full economic potential.

Buying additional GPUs simply to obtain more HBM is also inefficient. A customer may need more memory, not necessarily more compute.

NVIDIA's response is to expand beyond supplying the accelerator and address the infrastructure bottlenecks that limit its utilization.

NVIDIA CMX: Making Existing GPUs More Productive

NVIDIA's CMX Context Memory Storage Platform is designed to create a massive additional memory tier for AI inference.

The most immediately needed AI context remains in the GPU's fast HBM. Much larger amounts of KV cache can be moved to CMX systems using large-scale SSD capacity. NVIDIA's BlueField processors, Spectrum-X networking, and software manage the movement of that information between storage and compute.

The architecture can be simplified as:

GPU HBM → NVIDIA networking and BlueField → massive CMX SSD capacity

This could be particularly valuable for agentic AI. Thousands of AI agents may be active, waiting, or moving between tasks. Keeping every agent's entire context permanently in expensive HBM would be inefficient. CMX provides a lower-cost location for large amounts of context while allowing the system to retrieve it when needed.

NVIDIA has also created the STX reference architecture, allowing storage and system partners to build products around this architecture. NVIDIA can therefore provide critical processors, networking, software, and architecture while an ecosystem of partners builds the complete systems.

From a business perspective, this creates a potentially important new revenue opportunity:

NVIDIA can earn additional revenue by helping customers make their existing NVIDIA compute infrastructure more productive.

A Second Approach: Specialized Inference Compute

NVIDIA is simultaneously pursuing another strategy to improve inference economics: using specialized processors for workloads where a general-purpose GPU may not be the most efficient solution.

Its Groq 3 LPX architecture is designed to accelerate token generation.

The distinction is simple:

CMX addresses the memory bottleneck.
Groq 3 LPX addresses the token-generation bottleneck.
Together, these technologies illustrate a broader evolution in NVIDIA's strategy. Rather than expecting one GPU to perform every AI task, NVIDIA is moving toward an AI factory architecture in which different components are optimized for different workloads.

The future AI system may therefore include general-purpose GPUs, specialized inference processors, multiple memory tiers, high-speed networking, and software coordinating the entire system.

For NVIDIA, this creates an opportunity to capture a larger share of total AI infrastructure spending.

The Open Alternative: CXL—and Marvell's Opportunity

NVIDIA's proprietary CMX architecture is not the only approach to the AI memory problem.

Compute Express Link, or CXL, is an open industry standard that allows processors to access additional pools of memory. Instead of purchasing another GPU simply to obtain more memory capacity, a data center can potentially add shared DRAM that can be dynamically allocated among processors.

The key distinction is:

CMX is part of a tightly integrated NVIDIA ecosystem.

CXL is an open, vendor-agnostic standard supported by a broader semiconductor ecosystem.
One company positioned to participate in this opportunity is Marvell Technology (MRVL).

Marvell is developing CXL infrastructure through products such as Structera S switches and Structera X memory controllers. These technologies are designed to connect processors with larger pools of external memory and help expensive AI accelerators operate more efficiently.

Marvell's opportunity is also broader than CXL. The company participates across several important AI connectivity layers, including networking, custom silicon, optical connectivity, and technologies that connect memory and other resources to increasingly complex AI systems.

This creates an important second-order investment thesis:

NVIDIA may remain the dominant provider of AI compute, while companies such as Marvell can benefit from the increasing amount of connectivity required to keep that compute fully utilized.

CMX and CXL may compete in some workloads, but they could also coexist. A future AI memory hierarchy could potentially include:

HBM for the hottest data → CXL-attached DRAM for fast memory expansion → CMX-style SSD infrastructure for massive context storage

In such an architecture, NVIDIA benefits from controlling an increasingly integrated AI platform, while companies such as Marvell can benefit from the growth of the open connectivity and memory-expansion ecosystem.

The Bigger Picture

The AI industry is moving from an era focused primarily on capital deployment toward one increasingly focused on return on invested capital.

Hyperscalers have committed enormous amounts of money to AI infrastructure. Over time, investors will demand evidence that this infrastructure can generate revenue and attractive margins.

The winning AI infrastructure may therefore not simply be the system with the most GPUs or the greatest theoretical computing power. It may be the system that produces the greatest economic value from each dollar of capital and each megawatt of constrained power.

Tokens per watt is one way to measure that transition. The broader business objective is simpler: maximize AI output and revenue while minimizing the infrastructure required to produce it.

NVIDIA's CMX, specialized inference architecture, networking, and broader AI-factory strategy represent an attempt to address that challenge. At the same time, the emergence of CXL creates opportunities for companies such as Marvell that provide the connectivity required to make expensive AI compute more productive.

The next phase of AI infrastructure may therefore be less about how many GPUs can be installed and increasingly about how efficiently those GPUs—and the systems around them—can be monetized.

The investment opportunity may broaden from the companies that provide AI compute to the companies that make that expensive compute more productive.
