.... aaaand we already have an addendum which itself has been revised and adds critical nuance: 

Addendum (Revised): The Roadmap Disanalogy — Sequential Demand Regimes and the Recurrence of Payment

A.1 The claim, stated mechanically

Liang's capability roadmap — chain-of-thought reasoning → agents → continuous learning → self-iterating models → embodied intelligence — describes not one demand curve but a sequence of demand regimes, each with a distinct compute profile and each, so far, arriving with an order-of-magnitude jump in consumption. The naive version of the resulting bull case — "telecom served its purpose once, AI has future stages" — fails as history: fiber served many successive stages (Web 1.0, broadband, streaming, cloud backhaul, mobile data), and the internet's 2000-era roadmap was substantially correct. It saved no carrier, for two reasons: the stages arrived 5–15 years late relative to the debt, and — the deeper reason — the later stages consumed the same durable asset, so nobody was paid twice. The trench was bought once; every subsequent demand wave accrued to distressed-asset buyers and to users. The defensible claim is therefore mechanical, not narrative: in AI, successive demand regimes interact with short asset life — the feature Section II identified as the cycle's worst. When demand regimes succeed each other faster than fleets fully depreciate, and each regime demands a partially different hardware profile, the infrastructure suppliers are paid again at every stage. The 3-to-6-year silicon clock — unambiguously negative for capital owners — converts the supplier tiers into a multi-wave annuity no telecom equipment vendor ever experienced. Lucent sold the network once; if the roadmap cadence holds, the foundry, memory, packaging, and semicap complex sells the network every stage.

A.2 The stages, mapped to tiers

Stage 1 — reasoning (2024–26, realized). Test-time compute scaling turned inference from an afterthought into the dominant workload and drove the token explosion now visible in lab revenue. No longer a forecast; this is the demand that absorbed the 2024–25 capacity wave.

Stage 2 — agents (2025–27, arriving). Long-horizon autonomous tasks multiply tokens per task by orders of magnitude and shift the demand profile toward context: KV-cache, memory capacity, and the storage tier behind it — the dynamic behind the KV-warehouse architectures and the NAND rehabilitation in our technical thread, and behind Nvidia's own declaration that "the agentic AI inflection point has arrived." Beneficiary tiers: memory and storage (capacity even more than bandwidth), networking, power, and — noteworthy — the cheap-memory decode architectures, since agentic workloads are decode-dominated. Stage 2 is the designated absorber of the 2026–27 capacity wave; whether it fully shows up is precisely the coverage-ratio goalpost.

Stage 3 — continuous learning (undated; the B1 signpost). Deployed models that keep training blur the training/inference boundary and create a new compute class: every serving instance becomes a partial training instance. Previously flagged only as the dashboard-invalidating wildcard, it is properly two-sided: the chokepoint-map invalidator and the largest unpriced demand regime in the system — it could resurrect training-silicon and HBM demand just as decode-tier commoditization bites, or restructure the mix unrecognizably. It remains unsolved research (catastrophic forgetting is not an engineering detail), which is why it carries a signpost rather than a defensible probability.

Stage 3.5 — self-iterating models (gradual recursion; Liang insists on gradualism, not discontinuity). Models doing AI research: automated architecture and algorithm search, kernel and compiler generation, synthetic-data production, chip-design assistance, and eventually autonomous research agents running experimental programs — with continuous learning as its plausible prerequisite, since models must update from experience before productively iterating on themselves. Embryonic tracers already exist: AI-assisted chip floorplans, model-written kernels, rising fractions of frontier-lab code written by the labs' own models. This stage is the deepest disanalogy in the entire comparison: fiber never laid more fiber, and bandwidth never improved the productivity of bandwidth. Self-iteration makes AI infrastructure history's first recursive capital good — the infrastructure's output improves the infrastructure's own yield. The correct historical analogs are machine tools and semiconductors themselves (chips designing better chips via EDA), both of which sustained multi-decade capex supercycles on exactly that recursion; at this stage the telecom analogy does not degrade, it terminates. It is, however, the most violently two-sided stage on the roadmap. On one side, frontier research is compute-bound, so automating the researcher converts the binding constraint from scarce labor into pure compute, and the marginal ROI of a FLOP rises as models improve themselves — reflexive demand, the supplier annuity's strongest mechanism. On the other, self-iteration is the ultimate efficiency engine — the industrialization of exactly the algorithmic shocks (the KDA/K3 class) that deflate compute-per-capability. Which side dominates is the Jevons question at maximum stakes; every efficiency wave to date has expanded total consumption, but that is an observation, not a law. Two second-order effects belong on the record. First, recursion quietly undermines Liang's own commoditization doctrine: fast-follower economics depend on frontier ideas diffusing through papers and open weights, whereas self-iteration internalizes research into a private, compounding, non-publishing loop — a re-moating mechanism for whichever one or two labs sustain it, partially reversing the carrier-tier reclassification for the leaders while accelerating the squeeze-out of trailing, commitment-heavy labs. Second, Liang's gradualism is the least disinterested claim in his transcript: a discontinuity would strand his restraint strategy entirely, while gradualism is the only regime in which fast-following survives. His framing may be right; it should be weighted as testimony from a party whose strategy requires it.

Stage 4 — embodied intelligence (late-decade). Two distinct demand vectors, often conflated: edge inference silicon in the machines themselves (low-power SoCs, sensors — a supplier set largely outside today's data-center complex), and world-model training — video-scale data, fleet-scale simulation — the most compute-intensive workload class yet conceived, landing squarely on the existing big-cluster supply chain. The second vector is why embodied AI is a data-center bull case and not merely a robotics story.

A.3 What this does and does not rescue — the tier asymmetry

The roadmap is a supplier-tier bull case and an owner-tier complication, and keeping those separate is the whole discipline. For the silicon/memory/packaging/semicap complex, each stage transition is a new product cycle sold into a fleet that must be re-bought — the annuity structure, with Stage 3.5 as its strongest and reflexive installment. For the carrier tier, each transition is a stranding event: a leveraged landlord holding a fleet optimized for stage N faces accelerated economic obsolescence when stage N+1 shifts the profile — agents devaluing FLOPs-optimized fleets relative to context-optimized ones, continuous learning potentially devaluing both, recursion concentrating advantage in whichever customers own the loop rather than rent it. For the model layer, the roadmap bifurcates the tier: recursion re-moats the sustaining leaders while deepening the carrier logic for everyone behind them. For the hybrid tier, the roadmap is the strongest version of their option value — each stage a new monetization surface for entities owning distribution, and recursion compounding fastest where frontier compute and frontier models co-locate — with the risk concentrated entirely in inter-stage timing.

A.4 The binding constraint: cadence versus depreciation

The stress test the addendum must survive is the telecom epitaph itself: a correct roadmap does not prevent a bust, only a permanent one, and telecom's investors were wiped out inside a temporary one. The condition under which the roadmap functions as a bull case is precise: stage N+1 must arrive within the depreciation life of the stage-N fleet, and must be compute-expanding rather than compute-economizing. Both clauses are live risks. If agents plateau before continuous learning is solved, the 2027–28 energization wave meets a stage gap — an air-pocket — and embodied AI arriving gloriously in 2032 consoles the suppliers' next shareholders, exactly as the 2010s cloud consoled whoever bought Level 3's assets out of restructuring. And Liang's efficiency ideology cuts on the second clause: each stage could arrive in a compute-frugal form. The recursion of Stage 3.5 does not repeal any of this — a self-improvement loop that ignites in 2029 rescues nothing that breaks in 2027. The roadmap raises the bull case's ceiling — at Stage 3.5, dramatically — and does nothing, at any stage, to raise its floor.

A.5 Goalpost amendments

Seven additions to the dashboard and one reclassification. Agentic absorption metrics: tokens-per-task and deployed-agent utilization as they appear in lab and hyperscaler disclosures — Stage 2 is the designated absorber of the imminent capacity wave, and its sufficiency is checkable quarters before the coverage ratio resolves. World-model training runs: the first frontier-scale programs with disclosed compute budgets, the earliest tracer of Stage 4's data-center vector. Stage-transition stranding tell: the first leveraged compute landlord whose fleet reprices from profile mismatch rather than demand weakness — FLOPs-heavy capacity discounting while context-optimized capacity holds price — confirming the owner-tier risk mechanism empirically. Recursion tracers: AI-designed silicon or kernels entering production flows, disclosed fractions of model-generated lab code and training data, and the first autonomous research agents producing accepted novel results — checkable now, not in 2030. The re-moating tell: frontier labs going quieter — publication slowdowns, lengthening open-source capability lag, the DeepSeek-to-frontier gap widening rather than compressing — which doubles as the direct falsification test of Liang's gradualism-with-compression worldview, using his own company as the instrument. The Jevons readout: whether compute-per-frontier-capability trends flatten (recursion feeding demand) or steepen downward (recursion feeding deflation), readable in training-run disclosures. Reclassification: B1 (continuous learning) moves from wildcard to a two-sided entry — largest unpriced demand regime and chokepoint-map invalidator — with the research-milestone literature, not product announcements, as its leading indicator; it additionally becomes the gating signpost for Stage 3.5, so its resolution now carries two stages' weight.

A.6 Revised verdict

The analogy holds at the tier level, not the sector level — and it is now, explicitly, stage-conditional. The carrier role is occupied jointly by the leveraged neocloud/compute-landlord complex and the commitment-heavy frontier labs, and for that combined tier the 1999 script applies with full force on a credit-market clock centered on 2027 — with the roadmap adding a stranding mechanism (profile mismatch across stage transitions) the single-wave analysis lacked, and with one carve-out: any lab that sustains a self-iteration loop partially exits the carrier classification through re-moating, while the labs trailing it sink deeper into it. The Cisco role belongs to Nvidia and the scarcity-premium complex — real rents with an architectural expiry risk in 2027–28 — but the roadmap converts this tier's long-run character: under sustained stage cadence, the supplier complex is structurally closer to a recurring-repurchase annuity (machine tools, semicap) than to Lucent, because short asset life plus sequential regimes means the network is sold anew at every stage, a payment recurrence telecom never permitted. The integrated hyperscalers remain the unprecedented hybrid, downside capped at multiple compression, with the roadmap maximizing their option value at every stage and recursion compounding fastest inside their walls. China remains the seatless deflation exporter, compressing every timeline — and Stage 3.5 is the one development that could neutralize its export mechanism by ending the diffusion its fast-follower model requires. Nothing in the roadmap repeals the two unforgiving features of this cycle — inverted asset life and mid-cycle debt migration — nor the air-pocket risk that a real-but-late demand future is indistinguishable from a bubble at the moment the capacity wave breaks; the 2026–2027 observables retain full authority in every branch. What the roadmap changes is the meaning of surviving that window: telecom's survivors inherited a commodity; this cycle's survivors inherit a capital good that, from Stage 3.5 onward, participates in its own appreciation. Accordingly, the truth-content of the AI-infrastructure-versus-telecom comparison, previously fixed at ~50–55%, becomes a declining function of roadmap progress: ~50–55% if the roadmap stalls after agents, ~40% under sustained stage cadence through continuous learning, and converging toward ~10–15% — not zero, since the timing, leverage, and vendor-financing mechanics of 1999 remain valid warnings in every world — if self-iterating models arrive inside the depreciation window, because at that point the analogy's core premise, a passive commodity asset whose value migrates entirely to its users, is no longer merely weakened but structurally false.
