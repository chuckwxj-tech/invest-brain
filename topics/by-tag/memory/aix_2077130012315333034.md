# Dylan Patel of SemiAnalysis & $SMCI's Vik Malyala conversed on the Computex 2026 floor, and the recurring point was that the silicon is more ready than the buildings it goes in....

## 用户备注

来源: ai-x-discovery；机器初筛，仍需外部核查。

## 原文

Dylan Patel of SemiAnalysis & $SMCI's Vik Malyala conversed on the Computex 2026 floor, and the recurring point was that the silicon is more ready than the buildings it goes in. 

More key takeaways (save this):

1/ AMD's Helios rack weighs about 7,000 pounds, and Supermicro is AMD's launch partner for it (V.M.). Malyala's read on why customers are warming to a second vendor is sequencing, not AMD itself. Nvidia's rack-scale GB200 and GB300 did the expensive work of teaching operators how to take a liquid-cooled, rack-as-the-unit machine, so AMD gets to sell into a market Nvidia already normalized.

2/ Patel says the part AMD has not formally announced (D.P.): Helios is 72 GPUs, with more memory and more memory-FLOPs than Nvidia's Vera Rubin, "better in most every way," landing three to six months behind it. Public traction he names already includes Meta and Oracle, the latter for OpenAI. The trade is three to six months of lateness for a bigger-memory machine aimed at memory-starved buyers.

3/ Helios is roughly twice the width of a standard rack, and Malyala argues the odd form factor costs him nothing (V.M.). Neither Helios nor Vera Rubin fits a traditional data center anyway, so customers go purpose-built or retrofit, and there is no legacy-compatible option to prefer instead. One operator is rebuilding its doors to reel Helios in, which he calls trivial against a rack worth many millions.

4/ Nvidia's Vera Rubin looks like a cleaner ramp than the GB generation (V.M.). GB200 was the rough, brand-new design, GB300 smoothed it, and Rubin looks cleaner still. The binding limit has moved off the rack itself to whether a data center can deliver the power per rack, a property of the site and the grid that no chip roadmap can speed up.

5/ The AI-accelerator startups need an OEM willing to build a non-standard box rather than ship a cookie-cutter server (D.P./V.M.). Supermicro names d-Matrix, Positron, Furiosa AI and Rebellions as PCIe-accelerator customers, each wanting different topologies (single- vs multi-root complex) and, now, Arm host CPUs alongside Intel and AMD. The pitch is custom architecture plus global delivery at once, which no startup can fund on its own.

6/ PCIe switching is opening up (D.P.). Where Microchip and Broadcom held it, Marvell and Astera Labs now offer new switches at 256 and 320 lanes. More lane count is what lets a designer build a larger connected domain of storage, accelerators and NICs inside one rack, so the new entrants widen what topologies are buildable.

7/ Rack Ethernet is climbing 400 gig to 800 gig to about 1.6 terabit on Broadcom's Tomahawk 6, with UALink drawing real interest as an emerging interconnect (V.M.). Malyala's honest hedge is that adoption still has to be proven, tied to a design tension: build the fabric too specifically for one workload and the buyer is stuck, so it has to serve more than one.

8/ Storage went from afterthought to first-order problem (D.P.). Two drivers: KV-cache offload, an inference-era demand that spills attention state to fast storage instead of HBM, and the sharp rise in SSD cost. Storage bandwidth is now a serving-cost lever rather than a commodity line item.

9/ Nvidia's new CPUs, the Arm server CPU, and AMD's upcoming Venice are all converging on PCIe Gen 6 at once (V.M.). That unlocks the bandwidth behind Supermicro's PetaScale storage and CXL 3.0 memory-expansion devices in the same generation, packed into dense E1.S and E3 form factors in 1U and 2U.

10/ Hardware is only half the storage story (V.M.). Supermicro partners with software-defined storage vendors VAST, Weka, DDN, Qumulo and OS Nexus, and the point of naming five is that the customer does not get locked into a single traditional stack. The flexibility is sold as much as the drives.

11/ Cooling is the gate on Gen 6, and Supermicro leans on being first (V.M./D.P.). It was first to large-scale liquid cooling on xAI's Colossus, the first 100,000-GPU liquid-cooled deployment. The strategy is to pull more components under the loop over time, from processor and VRMs, to memory, now even E1.S drives, with bus-bar power delivery freeing space and improving airflow.

12/ Even the baseline system is a memory story before a compute story (V.M.). Supermicro's B300 HGX on Intel Granite Rapids-SP carries 32 DIMM slots, and with memory this expensive the slot count is what lets a buyer populate the box. Try to save money by loading only eight, and it "is not gonna work properly anyway."

Lastly. the tension under every layer is generality versus specialization. Malyala hits it at level after level, from the network fabric to the accelerators. Build too specifically for one workload and you're stuck with it, so it has to work for more than one.
