JPMorgan’s Supply Chain Update: The AI Boom’s Hidden Bottleneck Just Got Clearer

JPMorgan has raised its forecasts for TSMC’s CoWoS advanced packaging capacity after recent supply chain checks. However, the firm’s key conclusion is that a ~20% supply-demand gap is likely to persist into 2027–2028 because demand is outpacing even rapid capacity expansion.

> Capacity Outlook: 
This aligns with TSMC’s public guidance of >80% CAGR in CoWoS capacity from 2022–2027, though the company discloses limited granular details.
- $TSM ’s in-house CoWoS monthly capacity is now projected at ~115,000 wafers by end-2026, ~190,000 in 2027, and 220,000–225,000 by end-2028.
- OSAT providers ( $AMKR , ASE ) will add meaningful volume, rising from ~12-15k wafers/month in 2026 to ~85k by 2028.
- TSMC is shifting some SoIC resources to CoWoS to meet near-term needs.

> Why Shortages Remain? 
AI chips are becoming larger and more complex. Next-gen GPUs, AI CPUs, custom ASICs, and bigger packages consume significantly more packaging capacity per unit. $NVDA remains the biggest user, with modeled demand reaching ~1.735 million wafers annually by 2028 (including GPUs, Vera/Rosa CPUs, LPUs, and CPO products)

$AMD (Venice CPUs, MI450/MI500 accelerators)
$GOOG  (TPU v8/v9)
$AMZN  (Trainium3/4)

> Demand is broadening: 
This multi-customer pull, combined with higher per-chip consumption, keeps the market tight despite capacity growth. Some estimates (e.g., TrendForce) see the gap narrowing faster, but JPMorgan is more cautious on demand ramp and OSAT effectiveness.

JPMorgan keeps an Overweight rating on TSMC with a NT$3,100 target price, citing strong AI-driven growth (69% CAGR in data center revenue 2024–2029) and pricing power in advanced packaging.

Bottom line: Securing advanced packaging capacity is now just as important as securing leading-edge wafers.
